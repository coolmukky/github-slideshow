GOAL

How you complete it

This is a learning exercise, not a contest — teams aren't compared with each other. The aim is for every person to leave the hour better than they arrived.

The objective

Take a fictional company drowning in over-privileged autonomous AI agents and redesign its architecture around Zero Trust. You've completed it when an evaluator can look at your topology and your one-pager and clearly see identity, least privilege, continuous verification, and blast-radius control applied to every agent.

  • Every pain point in the dossier is addressed by a control in your design.
  • Each AI agent has its own identity and only the access it needs, only when it needs it.
  • Every agent-to-resource call passes through a policy checkpoint and is logged.
  • All three injections are answered inside your design, not bolted on after.

What you hand in

1
The Topology Diagram

A hand-drawn architecture on the canvas. Photographed to the clipboard and read by the AI proctor — so draw labelled boxes and clear arrows.

2
The Solution Sheet

A single page: the problem, your approach, key decisions, how the design answers each injection — plus a one-line individual reflection from each member. Uploaded and scored against the rubric.

Both are graded together. A great diagram with a thin write-up — or vice versa — caps your score.

OUTCOMES

What you'll take away

In one focused hour, you'll move from a messy real-world brief to a defensible design — and walk away with a mental model you can reuse in front of customers.

Understand

How Zero Trust applies to autonomous AI agents: per-agent identity, least privilege, a policy checkpoint on every call, blast-radius control, and audit by design.

Practise

Reading an ambiguous brief, choosing an architecture as a team, deciding when the brief changes, and communicating the design clearly under time pressure.

Walk away with

A reusable eight-control reference pattern for agentic security — and the habit of defending design trade-offs out loud, not just drawing them.

If you're a team of three, the Conductor also runs the Scribe's standing job; with five, add a second Critic. The one rule that keeps you on track

How time-checking works

  1. Use one timer everyone can see — a phone or laptop counting up from 00:00. Every time in this playbook is "minutes elapsed."
  2. The Conductor owns the clock and is the only person watching it closely, so the others can focus on the work.
  3. At each checkpoint, the Conductor says the time out loud — e.g. "We're at 20:00, moving to Decide." So the whole team always knows where the clock is.
  4. The full list of what to say and when is on the Conductor's time-cue cheat sheet at the end of this card. Red lines on it are injection reveals.
  5. When a phase's hard-stop time hits, stop and move on — even if you're not finished. An unfinished phase beats an unsubmitted exercise.
If two people want the same role

Rock-paper-scissors, or the person who has done it least before takes it — this is practice, so stretch into the unfamiliar. Every role carries equal weight.

TWISTS

The injections

Sealed until their moment. Each one is a real decision under pressure — the Conductor reads it aloud, the team has roughly three minutes to decide, and the answer must show up in your design or your sheet. They exist to see how you think when the brief changes. All three are always used, so every team is scored on the same twists.

01 · Autonomous Expansion
@ 24:00

A new reconciliation agent must read and write the production customer ledger with no human in the loop. Leadership wants it live this week.

Decide together
How do you grant this access under Zero Trust without standing, broad privilege? Look for: per-agent identity, just-in-time scoped tokens, policy-gated writes, and human approval reserved for the riskiest actions.
02 · Token in the Wild
@ 38:00

Monitoring fires: one agent's credential is calling APIs from an unexpected region at 20× normal volume. It may be compromised right now.

Decide together
How does your design contain the blast radius in real time? Look for: short token lifetimes, instant revocation, continuous verification, segmentation that stops lateral movement, and behavioural anomaly response.
03 · Prove It
@ 50:00

An auditor demands a complete, tamper-evident trail of every agent-to-resource action for the last 90 days — produced on demand.

Decide together
Does your design already produce this, and where? Look for: logging at the policy checkpoint, identity-bound records, immutable/append-only storage, and a single place to query it.

Confidential · Internal Architecture Review ZERO TRUST · TARGET STATE

Halcyon Pay

Halcyon Pay is a mid-size fintech that recently deployed three autonomous AI agents into production: a support-resolution agent that reads customer records and issues refunds, a reconciliation agent that moves data between the ledger and partner banks, and a fraud-triage agent that queries transactions and freezes accounts.

Today, each agent authenticates once with a shared service account and is then trusted to act broadly across internal APIs, databases, and SaaS tools. The network is flat, credentials are long-lived, and there's no record of which agent did what. Leadership has mandated a move to Zero Trust before the next agent ships. Your team designs the target-state architecture.

Pain points to surface

  • Agents share one over-privileged service account — no per-agent identity.
  • Implicit trust after first login; no re-verification per request.
  • Flat network lets a compromised agent move laterally.
  • Static, long-lived credentials that are never rotated.
  • No policy checkpoint authorising individual agent actions.
  • No audit trail tying actions to a specific agent.
  • Tool and prompt-injection misuse with no runtime guardrails.
  • No human-in-the-loop on high-risk actions (refunds, freezes).

EXAMPLE

A worked solution, end to end

Here's what a strong team produces for the Halcyon Pay scenario — a labelled topology and a completed solution sheet. Use it to calibrate, not to copy: your scenario specifics will differ. The numbers ①–⑧ on the diagram map to the eight reference controls in the Debrief.

Sample topology — Halcyon Pay target state

Read it as a flow: agents authenticate with their own identity, the gateway issues a scoped, short-lived token per action after consulting a deny-by-default policy, calls reach only their microsegment, high-risk actions need a human, anomalies trigger revocation, and everything lands in an immutable audit log.

Sample solution sheet — completed

Problem in one sentence

Halcyon Pay's three autonomous agents share one over-privileged account on a flat network with no per-agent audit, so a single compromised agent could read, move, or alter customer funds undetected.

Approach

Re-architect around Zero Trust: give every agent its own identity, broker every action through a policy enforcement point that issues just-in-time least-privilege tokens, isolate resources into microsegments, gate irreversible actions behind a human, and log every call to an immutable, identity-bound trail.

Key decisions & why
  • Per-agent workload identity over a shared service account — every action is attributable and individually revocable.
  • JIT, scoped, short-lived tokens over static keys — a leaked token expires fast and unlocks only one action.
  • One gateway/PEP + deny-by-default policy — a single place to authorize, guardrail, and log every call.
  • Microsegmentation between ledger, records, and partner API — contains blast radius, blocks lateral movement.
  • Human-in-the-loop on refunds, freezes, and production writes — keep autonomy without ceding irreversible actions.
Injection 01 · Autonomous expansion

Give the reconciliation agent its own identity and a token scoped to "ledger read + one write," issued per task and expiring in minutes; production writes still route through human approval. No standing broad access.

Injection 02 · Token in the wild

The monitor flags the abnormal region and volume, the gateway revokes that agent's token instantly, and short TTLs plus microsegmentation cap what the attacker reaches in the seconds before revocation.

Injection 03 · Prove it

Every agent→resource call is already logged at the gateway, identity-bound and append-only, so the 90-day trail is a single query — nothing to reconstruct.

  • Name the injection that was hardest and why it stretched the design.

Target state · the pattern you were aiming at

Reference design at a glance

  1. Per-agent identity — each agent has its own verifiable identity; no shared service accounts.
  2. Just-in-time, scoped, short-lived tokens — least privilege per action, expiring fast.
  3. Policy checkpoint on every call — authorize each agent→resource request; deny by default.
  4. Microsegmentation — isolate agents so a compromise can't move laterally.
  5. Continuous verification + anomaly monitoring — re-check trust each request; revoke fast.
  6. Runtime guardrails on agent tools — constrain tools and inputs to blunt prompt-injection.
  7. Human-in-the-loop on high-risk actions — refunds, freezes, production writes.
  8. Immutable, identity-bound audit log at the checkpoint — queryable on demand.

During the hour

  • Start every team's timer together; note the official start time.
  • Float between tables — don't answer design questions; redirect to the rubric.
  • If a team stalls, prompt with a question, never an answer.
  • Check injection reveals land near 24:00 / 38:00 / 50:00.
  • At 60:00, confirm both artifacts are submitted before scoring.

After the clock

  • Run the 10-minute debrief and reveal the reference design.
  • Score with the rubric; route low-confidence diagrams to an SME.
  • Capture one improvement per team; name common gaps across tables.
  • Keep strong sample diagrams and sheets as anchors for next time.
